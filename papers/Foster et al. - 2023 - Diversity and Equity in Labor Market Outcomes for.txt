Abstract
The lack of gender and racial diversity in economics academia is well-established, but less is known about the overall placement
and earnings of economists. Using person-level data from the Survey of Earned Doctorates linked to Longitudinal
Employer-Household Dynamics jobs data, we examine placements and earnings for new PhD economists by gender and race.
Female PhD economists earn 12 percent less than their male counterparts; compared to their white counterparts on average
Black PhD economists earn 15 percent less and overall underrepresented minority PhD economists earn 8 percent less. Pay
disparities are attenuated in some sectors and when controlling for PhD granting institution and employer.
Keywords Gender · Race · Market for economists · Diversity

Introduction
A large and growing literature documents the lack of gender
and racial diversity in economics over multiple dimensions
from choice of undergraduate major, to graduate field,
to post-PhD placement, and promotion in academia.1 The
2022 annual report from the Committee on the Status of
Women in the Economics Profession finds that 17.8 percent
of full economics professors at PhD-granting institutions are
women (CSWEP 2022). Similarly, the 2021 annual report
from the Committee on the Status of Minority Groups in the
Economics Profession shows that 7.7 percent of full-time
full economics professors at PhD-granting institutions are
from minority groups (Black, Hispanic, and Native American)
(CSMGEP 2021). The American Economic Association
(AEA) and others have taken actions to increase diversity in
the profession, but greater diversity remains an elusive goal.2

Only a third of economics PhDs are awarded to women,
a share unchanged since the 1990s, and economics ranks
near the bottom in the share of doctorates in social sciences
awarded to underrepresented minority students.3 We expand
our understanding of the state of the profession beyond academia
by combining individual-level data on PhD economists
with jobs data to document placement and earnings in
economics across multiple sectors of the economy.
Our ability to look beyond academia provides a more
complete view of the economics profession and suggests
that institutional barriers to advancement for underrepresented
groups vary by sector. Recognizing the systemic
issues within the economics profession, we present placement
and earnings results without covariates as the baseline.
We then extend these results to look at earnings by
sector and control for PhD program rank and post-PhD
employer.
Our contribution to this literature rests on the breadth
of our data on placements and earnings post-PhD attainment.
We match data on PhD economists from the
U.S. Survey of Earned Doctorates (SED) to a national
database of jobs, tracking economists’ placements and
earnings in their early post-PhD careers. We provide a
detailed look at the careers of about 12,500 economists
not only in academia but also in government and industry.
This wider focus is especially important as the share
of PhD economists’ placements in academia is falling
while the share in industry is rising. As one example,
Athey and Luca (2019) report that Amazon employs
more economists than the largest economics academic
departments (in terms of scale, Amazon hired over 150
economists between 2014–2019).
Our matched data provide a unique opportunity to survey
the U.S. labor market for PhD economists, representing
an expansion in size and scope relative to existing
work. Bedard et al. (2021) summarize the current literature
as “all existing research relies on cross-sectional
data, short early career panels, or panels specific to a
narrow set of institutions (p. 69).” Boustan and Langan
(2019) use CSWEP survey information on 88 PhD programs
to examine job placement rates by gender at PhD
granting institutions, other academic institutions, private
sector jobs, public sector jobs, and jobs outside the U.S.
They find similar average placement rates for men and
women at PhD granting institutions and public sector
jobs, but higher placement rates for women in other academic
and private sector jobs.
We build upon our earlier work which focuses on PhD
economists in the federal government (Foster et al. 2020).
In regressions controlling for experience and agency effects,
we find earnings gaps relative to white males for females
ranging from 1 percent for white and Asian economists to
6 percent for URM economists. For males, the earnings
gaps relative to white males range from 6 percent for Asian
economists to 10 percent for URM economists. Wessel et al.
(2021) also focus on PhD economists in the federal government.
They combine data from multiple sources to create a
sample of about 2,300 PhD economists representing about
95% of the PhD economists in the federal government and
report 26% of federal PhD economists identify as minorities
and 29% as women in 2020.
Our paper is related to Schultz and Stansbury (2022),
which uses the public SED,4 and finds economics is less
socioeconomically diverse than other PhD major fields:
economists are the least likely to be first-generation college
students and one of the most likely to have at least one parent
with a graduate degree.5 Further, the share of first-generation
students in top economics programs is especially small,
about 5 percent, compared to 17 percent in programs below
the top 15. As Jones and Sloan (2021) show, most tenuretrack
faculty at PhD-granting institutions in the U.S. come
from the top 15 PhD programs. These faculty are drawn
from a population that is even less diverse than the profession
generally. Our descriptive results on the role of PhD
program rank in determining post-PhD earnings complement
this work.
We briefly highlight research concerning reasons underlying
this lack of diversity in order to provide context for our
analyses. Much of the work with respect to gender uses a
“leaky pipeline” analogy to illustrate the pattern of fewer
and fewer women at each stage of an academic career.
Kolpin and Singell Jr (1996) find women have lower initial
placements than similarly qualified men, as indicated by
their subsequent productivity. Ginther and Kahn (2004) find
female economists are less likely to get tenure at their first
academic job, even when controlling for publication record
and family characteristics. An important component of hiring
and promotion is publication. Hengel (2022) finds less
than 8 percent of authors in the top four economic journals
over 1950–2015 are female and female authors are held to

Once published, economics papers with female authors
are less likely to be cited by related papers (Koffi 2021),

Turning to race and ethnicity,6 Price (2009) finds when
the supply of Black economists increased, the number of
Black economics professors remained unchanged; thus, he
concludes the challenge for Black economists is not only
a “pipeline” but a “color-line.” Darity and Kreeger (2014)
and Simms and Conrad (2019) take a case-study approach
as they examine the experiences of Black economics graduate
students at MIT and Stanford University, respectively,
and note the importance of institutional support. In a recent
survey of minorities in economics, the most commonly
cited challenges with advancement in the profession were
a lack of mentoring and role models, and a sense of isolation
(Bayer et al. 2020). Several respondents also cited a
lack of information regarding non-academic opportunities
for economists, with students from blue-collar backgrounds
indicating greater interest in non-academic jobs.
Logan and Myers Jr (2020) discuss the importance of
networks in building citations and note the Review of
Black Political Economy was not indexed in the Journal
of Economic Literature for decades. Doleac et al. (2021)
note the importance of presentations and find underrepresented
minority economists accounted for only 1.1 percent
of invited seminar speakers in their sample covering
2014–2019. Dupas et al. (2021) find the underrepresentation
of economists by race and ethnicity (and sexuality and disability
status) at top economic departments precludes them
from including these characteristics in their analysis of
seminar and conference culture since they “would lack the
statistical power to say much of interest” (p. 4).
In sum, while much of this research focuses on supplyside
issues of underrepresentation, understanding demandside
factors is also important, as improvements in the supply
side by diversifying the pipeline may not be enough to
improve diversity in the profession. ‘Demand-side’ factors,
such as implicit bias in placements and promotions, further
hamper efforts to improve equity and inclusion within the
profession.
Data and Matching Methodology
Survey of Earned Doctorates
Our data on PhD economists come from the Survey of
Earned Doctorates (SED) produced by the National Center
for Science and Engineering Statistics (NCSES). The SED
is an annual census of all individuals receiving a research
doctorate from an accredited U.S. institution in a given academic
year. Due to the availability of comprehensive lists of
doctorate-granting institutions and the institutions' high levels
of participation in the survey, coverage of PhD-granting
departments is nearly complete. Graduate schools collect the
survey data from degree recipients at the time of doctorate
completion, and response rates are over 90 percent. Thus, the
SED data provide nearly complete coverage of all individuals
who received an economics PhD in the U.S. Our subset
of these data covers 2001–2017.
We use the following SED variables: doctoral degree
received, year of receipt, granting institution, field of study,
gender, citizenship, ethnicity, and race. Two important
features of the SED are (1) race and ethnicity are reported
only for U.S. citizens and permanent residents and (2) all
responses for Hispanic ethnicity are reported as Hispanic
only (thus only non-Hispanic responses are further categorized
by race).7 We use the PhD granting institution from
the SED to create PhD program fixed effects, to help capture
the significance of hierarchies in the economics profession
with its emphasis on rank of PhD granting institutions, rank
of employment institution, number of citations, and placement
of publications in top 5 journals (Fourcade et al. 2015;

Although our analysis uses individual-level data from the
SED, we start with SED published figures to provide context.
There are about one thousand economics PhD recipients per
year over our sample period (rising from about 900 in 2001 to
about 1,200 in 2017). Since our analysis is post-PhD and thus
encompasses the entirety of pipeline issues, a relevant comparison
group is the U.S. adult population.8 Thus, we present
total adult population shares by way of crude comparison:
women are 51.0 percent of the general population, but only
34 percent of economics Ph.D. recipients; American Indian
and Alaska Native (AIAN) individuals are 1.1 percent of the
population, but less than half a percent of economists; Asian
individuals are 6.1 percent of the population, but 16 percent
of economics PhD recipients; Black individuals are 12 percent
of the population, but only 4 percent of new economists;
Hispanic individuals are16.8 percent of the population, but
only 7 percent of economist PhD recipients; Native Hawaiian
and Pacific Islander (NHPI) are 0.2 percent and are pooled
with “other race or race not reported” in the SED; and White
individuals are 64.1 percent of the general population and 63
percent of the economist PhD recipient population.9
By way of further comparison, gender parity of PhD
recipients is already achieved (and surpassed) for some fields
outside of economics. For example in 2017, 53 percent of
PhD recipients in biological and biomedical sciences are
women and 61 percent in sociology are women (NCSES
(2018), table 16). Overall 47 percent of PhD recipients
across all fields are female in 2017. However, this is not the
case for racial parity which is not achieved across all fields;
for example in 2017 Hispanic and Black PhD recipients each
account for only 7 percent of all PhD recipients (NCSES
(2018) Table 22 and see Appendix B in Foster et al. (2022)).
When using the micro data, we expand the definition
of the economics field used in SED public tabulations to
include agricultural and environmental economists. The
impact of this broader definition is documented in the
Appendix where we discuss detailed measurement issues.
Longitudinal Employer‑Household Dynamics Data
Our data on earnings and employment after the PhD are
primarily from the U.S. Census Bureau’s Longitudinal
Employer-Household Dynamics (LEHD) data. LEHD data
includes quarterly earnings records collected by state unemployment
insurance (UI) programs, linked to establishmentlevel
data from the Quarterly Census of Employment and
Wages (QCEW) data. LEHD employment coverage is quite
broad, covering over 95 percent of private-sector workers
and almost all state and local government employment (see
Abowd et al. (2009) for details).
We use LEHD data for all private-sector and publicsector
employers in all available states, from 2001 to 2017.
As the LEHD wage record data have some coverage gaps
(particularly federal workers) we supplement the LEHD
data with Internal Revenue Service (IRS) form W-2 data to
obtain earnings for jobs not covered in LEHD data. These
two data sources together provide near-universal coverage
of U.S. earnings. Information on placements/employers for
economists comes from the QCEW.
We aggregate earnings from all jobs held during the
year for our measure of earnings. Our earnings data include
wage and salary earnings, bonuses, overtime, and incentive
pay, but exclude any self-employment earnings, which usually
includes income from consulting, patents, prizes, or
book royalties. The primary employer is the highest paying
employer for each individual in each year, which we use to
determine the sector of employment. All earnings measures
in this paper are real earnings, measured in 2015 dollars.
Matched SED/LEHD Sample
We use personal identifiers called Protected Identification
Keys (PIKs) assigned to individuals by the Census Bureau’s
Person Identification Validation System (PVS) to link SED
respondents to LEHD and W-2 earnings histories following
graduation. Our sample of PhD economists is therefore different
from the SED in two important respects. First, we cannot
link earnings records for SED respondents who are not
assigned a PIK. Our SED microdata contain degree records
for approximately 20,500 PhD economists. We can assign a
PIK to approximately 19,000 (93 percent).
Second, we can only characterize post-PhD earnings for
those economists who work in the U.S. after graduation.
This restriction further narrows our sample to 12,500 PhD
economists (61 percent). Thus, when comparing our data to
the SED, it is important to note that our sample is less than
two-thirds of all economics PhDs granted by U.S. institutions.
The majority of economics PhDs granted by U.S. institutions
are awarded to temporary visa holders. Since most
leave the country after receiving their degrees, we are unable
to match them to our earnings data after they leave the U.S.
Presumably, many of these temporary visa holders obtained
post-PhD employment outside of the U.S. But many remain
in the U.S. for work following graduation. Economists who

Gender, Ethnicity, and Race Measurement
As is common in this literature (including in the SED),
we use all respondents when stratifying by gender, but
for results by race and ethnicity we use only the subset of
doctorate recipients who are U.S. citizens or permanent
residents at the time of graduation.11 We recognize these
groups do not encompass all notions of diversity in the
profession, especially as almost half of economists working
in the U.S. are foreign-born. The unique challenges facing
foreign-born economists working in the U.S. are worthy
of further study but are beyond the scope of our analysis.
Due to small sample sizes, we cannot report results
separately for AIAN and NHPI economists and thus they
appear in a combined category with Hispanic economists.
We also pool those who report two or more races and those
who report no race, both very small categories. In some
analyses it is necessary to further group race and ethnicity
into an underrepresented minority (URM) category
which combines AIAN, Black, Hispanic, NHPI, two or
more races, and race not reported respondents. Ideally, we
would be able to produce statistics for all race/ethnicity
categories separately to capture the unique experiences
of each group. Further, it would be ideal if we could also
produce statistics that also take into account the importance
of gender and for all race categories together but
are constrained in our ability to provide a detailed intersectional
analysis.12
Employment and Earnings of PhD
Economists
We start by providing employment results across all sectors
where we find low shares of female and underrepresented
minority economists in our sample of economists
who received their PhD between 2001–2017. The first two
columns of Table 1 show the demographics of the full set
of PhD economists in the SED and of our sample of PhD
economists.13 Approximately one-third are female. Temporary
residents account for 61 percent of the full SED
sample, but only 45 percent of PhD economists in our sample,
primarily due to the large proportion of temporary
residents who do not work in the U.S. after PhD receipt.
Within the remaining category of U.S. citizens and permanent
residents where we can classify race and ethnicity,
AIAN, Hispanic, and NHPI account for 5 percent, Asian
for 12 percent, Black for 4 percent, and White for 76 percent
of our sample of economists.
We categorize employers into three major sectors: academia,
government, and industry.14 In columns 3–5 of
Table 1 we show a cross-section of all economists working
in the U.S. in 2017, aggregating across years of PhD
receipt.15 The first row of Table 1 shows the shares of
women employed in academia, industry, and government
are 33 percent, 36 percent, and 38 percent respectively.
Thus, the overall lack of diversity by gender is slightly
less pronounced in government and more pronounced in
academia.
The share of temporary visa holders varies across sectors
(almost half of industry economists, less than 20 percent
of government), so race is reported here as the share
of U.S. citizens/residents to make comparisons across
columns easier. We start with the combined category of
AIAN, Hispanic, and NHPI economists. These account
for roughly 5 percent across all three categories with academia
only slightly higher than industry and government.
Asian economists account for 9 percent in academia,
17 percent in industry, and 14 percent in government.
Black economists represent 4 percent of economists in
academia, 3 percent in industry, and 5 percent in government.
Finally, white economists account for 77 percent
in academia, 71 percent in industry, and 76 percent in
government. Thus, the lack of diversity by race and ethnicity
appears to be pervasive across all three sectors.

specification, but with both sets of fixed effects, Asian economists
earn 2 percent less than white economists.
Using the regression with program and employer controls,
the largest pay gaps in the economics profession are
for women and Black men, who both make 10 percent less
than their white male colleagues controlling for experience,
PhD program, and employer. Graduates of top programs
working in the U.S. are slightly less likely to be female or
URM, and more likely to be Asian.19 This distribution is
partly why controlling for program effects generally lowers
wage gaps for women and URM economists but raises them
for Asian economists. While earnings gaps are larger in the
baseline specification, the overall picture of larger wage gaps
for women and Black men remains the same. In this pooled
regression the program and employer fixed effects appear to
explain very little of the earnings gap for women, somewhat
more the earnings gaps by race, but the amount sorting into
PhD program and employer matters for earnings differences
varies by sector, as we describe next.
Earnings within Sectors
In our regressions by sector, we must pool AIAN, Black,
Hispanic, NHPI economists, and those indicating multiple
races into an underrepresented minority (URM) group due
to small cells. Collapsing these groups flattens the heterogeneity
in wage gaps by race we see in Table 2, with URM
economists having an 8 percent wage gap in the baseline
and a 5 percent wage gap in the full fixed effect specification
(see Appendix Table 4 for the full set of estimates). In
interpreting our results by sector, it is important to keep in
mind effects are likely heterogeneous across groups within
this category.
Table 3 shows regression estimates for economists in
academia. Starting with the upper panel results, on average,

in academia (column 1). Controlling for PhD program
lowers these gaps slightly (column 2) and controlling for
sorting across employers lowers the gap slightly more (column

and both program and employer fixed effects, the wage gap
for women in academia is 12 percent (column 4). On average,
URM economists earn 9 percent less than their white
colleagues with similar experience in academia (column 1).
The gap for URM economists relative to white economists is
5 percent when we add program and employer fixed effects.
There is no significant difference in earnings for Asian economists
relative to white economists until we add in either
program or employment (or both) fixed effects which then
lead to a 6 percent earnings gap relative to white economists.
The results for race by gender stratification in the lower

URM men; the coefficient on earnings of URM women is

this premium (in baseline and in regressions with

Table 4 shows these same regression estimates for economists

Adding program and employer effects, the earnings

Asian economists face an earnings gap in industry

when adding in program and employer fixed effects.

Summing up the results from Tables 3–5, on average,
women earn 15 percent less than men with similar experience
in academia, 12 percent less in industry, and 9 percent
less in the public sector.20 Controlling for PhD program lowers
these gaps slightly (top PhD programs skew male) but
controlling for sorting across employers lowers the gap by
more. In our specification controlling for work experience
and both program and employer fixed effects, the wage gaps
for women in academia, industry, and government are 12 percent,
9 percent, and 4 percent, respectively. In other words,
sorting of women to less well-paying employers accounts for
over half the gender wage gap within government, one-fourth
of the gap in industry, and two-tenths of the gap in academia.
Academia has both the largest pay gap for women, and the
largest share of that gap that is unexplained after controlling
for PhD program, employer, and experience.
URM economists earn, on average, 9 percent less than white
economists with similar experience in academia, 10 percent less
in industry, and 4 percent less in the public sector. When we
control for PhD program and employer, these gaps are 5 percent
in academia, 3 percent in industry, and 5 percent in government.
That is, PhD program and employer sorting explain almost
one-half of the wage gap in academia (9 versus 5 percent) and
more than one-half in industry (10 versus 3 percent) but have
little impact in government. In the race and gender regressions
controlling for experience, program, and employer fixed
effects, the URM penalty is driven by male economists: male
URM economists earn about 6 percent less than their white
male counterparts in academia, 2 percent less in industry, and
8 percent less in government. The overall earnings premium for
Asian economists (3 percent) is driven by the public sector (4
percent) as there is no significant difference in academia and
there is an earnings gap in industry (3 percent). The impact of
being a female Asian economist on earnings in the baseline case
varies over the sectors from negative in academia (15 percent)
to positive in government (5 percent).

Acknowledging the issues with diversity along the entire
pipeline, we need to be careful in how we interpret results
that control for PhD program and employer. As one of our
colleagues aptly put it “the road to earnings inequality
is long” and parsing out the impact of each stage on the
earnings gap is beyond the scope of this paper. We have
attempted to describe one part of this long road, but a full
accounting would include the entire educational experience,
as well as other socioeconomic factors including health and
access to wealth. Moreover, we acknowledge that our measures
of race only crudely capture the lived experience of
underrepresented economists.21
Our ability to combine the rich data collected in the Survey
of Earned Doctorates with the near universe of employment
and earnings from the Longitudinal Employer-Household
Dynamics dataset has enabled us to provide a view into the
labor market dynamics of the economics profession for PhD
economists. With our linked sample, we provide a unique
view into the employment and earnings of PhD economists
by gender and race. We hope our contribution of new descriptive
statistics provides a useful framework for other research
into diversity, equity, and inclusion in our profession.
